In our hometown, my grandfather owned a body shop. He owned it for 47 years. My dad ran it for a while but gave it back when we moved to Arkansas. I wasn't too involved in the shop growing up--how could I have been at <= 12 years old? Regardless, I grew up around cars and body work. My dad is also now an instructor for Pilot. So writing estimates in college was just something that was almost a given for my brother and I.

My job at Pilot Catastrophe was my first real, big boy job. I had many jobs up to that point, like all kids do, but they were the usual kid jobs: bus boy, fast food, etc. I remember when my brother and I got the call that we were going to work. In Colorado (we lived in Alabama). I was terrified. We were employed as automobile adjusters. So I was moving from serving people chicken and taking out the trash to being in charge of claims worth potentially hundreds of thousands of dollars. None of mine ever cost that much, but it was still a big jump.

Hunter graduated the next year and joined the Navy, but I didn't, so I was stuck working for Pilot. It was ok, but I hated not knowing what I wanted to do. What I did know was that I didn't want to do this forever. Regardless, I was good at it, and as I started to get more and more into computers, leadership noticed and moved me to a technical leadership position: Technical Advisor. I was essentially in charge of teams of adjusters, responsible for every part of them, from how they dressed to their customer service to the functionality of their estimating system. It was a big job, and a diverse one, and boy it was a stressful one too. Trying to keep my bosses happy, the customers happy, the shops happy, and trying to teach the adjusters while also being stern was a lot to juggle. But it taught me a hell of a lot--it still is. I still work for Pilot, although it's on a contract basis. If they need me for a big storm, I suppose I'll go. Other than that, I can't say I'm too eager to do anything other than write computer programs. I love that shit.
